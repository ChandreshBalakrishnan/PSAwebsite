Electrical Diverging and Converging

The electrical subsystem design followed a diverging and converging methodology, focusing on control logic architecture, button configuration, and circuit layout organization. The diverging phase explored multiple interaction and layout strategies to identify limitations and opportunities for improved usability and reliability.

Electrical divergence began with an evaluation of control logic architectures, including sequential cycling control and finite-state machine (FSM)-based control. Sequential cycling offered a simple implementation but required inefficient navigation through multiple states to reach a desired mode. FSM-based control was explored as an alternative, explicitly defining system states and transitions to improve predictability and clarity.

Button configurations were also varied during divergence. A single-button input scheme minimized hardware complexity but limited interaction efficiency and restricted state navigation. A two-button input scheme was investigated to support bidirectional navigation and more intuitive user control, despite requiring additional wiring and circuit complexity.

Circuit layout organization further diverged between distributed wiring layouts and structured, organized arrangements. Distributed layouts allowed flexibility during early prototyping but reduced readability, increased the likelihood of wiring errors, and made reproduction difficult. Structured layouts emphasized functional grouping, minimized wire crossings, and improved overall clarity. This analysis was carried out using the engineering design methods of SCAMPER and Classical Brainstorming.

Converging Notes:

Selection of FSM-Based Control Logic

The finite-state machine (FSM) architecture was selected as the final control strategy. Sequential cycling control was not chosen due to its inefficiency in state navigation and limited interaction capability.

The FSM approach provided clear state definitions and deterministic transitions, aligning with the project requirement for predictable system behavior. Although it introduced additional software complexity, the benefits in usability and system clarity justified this trade-off.

Selection of Two-Button Control Scheme

The two-button input configuration was selected as the final design choice. The single-button approach was rejected due to its inability to support efficient bidirectional navigation between states.

The two-button scheme enabled users to transition between any system states with minimal interaction, directly supporting the FSM architecture. Despite increasing hardware complexity, this configuration significantly improved user experience and control efficiency.

Selection of Structured Circuit Layout

A structured and symmetrical circuit layout was selected as the final electrical configuration. Distributed wiring layouts were rejected due to reduced readability and reproducibility.

The structured layout improved debugging efficiency, minimized wiring errors, and facilitated replication of the circuit design. Furthermore, maintaining a flat and organized component arrangement enhanced both aesthetic coherence and mechanical stability within the enclosure.

Despite requiring additional planning, this layout best satisfied the design objectives of clarity, reliability, and reproducibility.
